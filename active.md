## Introduction

Superstore is a leading retail organization that operates across multiple regions in the United States. The company specializes in selling furniture, office supplies, and technology products through both physical retail locations and online channels. With a vast network of stores and a comprehensive product catalog, Superstore generates significant amounts of transactional data daily. This data encompasses customer information, product details, sales transactions, shipping records, and store performance metrics. The organization faces the challenge of effectively leveraging this data to improve decision-making processes across various business functions including inventory management, customer segmentation, regional performance analysis, and trend identification. By implementing a robust data warehouse solution, Superstore can consolidate disparate data sources into a unified repository that supports advanced analytics and business intelligence activities. The data warehouse enables stakeholders to perform complex queries, generate insightful reports, and visualize key performance indicators through dashboards. This report documents the design and implementation of a comprehensive data warehouse solution using a Galaxy Schema approach, the ETL pipeline development using Apache NiFi, and the creation of interactive Power BI visualizations to derive actionable business intelligence from the Superstore dataset.

## Design and Implementation of a Data Warehouse

### Identify Data Sources

The data warehouse implementation for Superstore utilizes three distinct types of data sources to capture the full breadth of organizational data. These sources were carefully selected to represent the different data formats and storage mechanisms typically found in retail environments, ensuring comprehensive data integration for analytical purposes. The first data source consists of CSV files that contain dimension data including customer demographics, product catalog information, store locations, shipping modes, and date dimension values. These CSV files serve as the foundation for dimension tables and include customers.csv with 4,911 customer records containing CustomerID, CustomerName, Segment, Country, City, State, PostalCode, and Region fields. The products.csv file contains 1,894 product records with ProductID, Category, SubCategory, and ProductName fields. Additional CSV files include DimDate.csv with calendar date information spanning 2014 to 2016, DimStore.csv with 4 store locations, and DimShipMode.csv with 4 shipping method options. The second data source is an SQLite database file (superstore.db) that serves as the operational data store containing the SalesFact table with 9,994 transaction records. This database contains the fact table with fields including RowID, OrderID, OrderDate, ShipDate, ShipMode, CustomerID, ProductID, Sales, Quantity, Discount, and Profit. The SQLite database provides efficient storage and querying capabilities for transactional data while maintaining referential integrity with dimension tables through foreign key relationships. The third data source comprises Excel workbook files including sales_generated.xlsx, products_generated.xlsx, and customers_generated.xlsx. These Excel files provide alternative data representations that can be imported for additional analysis and serve as backup data sources. The Excel format enables easy data sharing with external stakeholders and provides a familiar interface for data exploration and preliminary analysis before incorporating data into the warehouse.

### Design the Data Warehouse Schema

The data warehouse implements a Galaxy Schema design, also known as a fact constellation schema, which represents a more complex data warehouse architecture that accommodates multiple fact tables sharing common dimension tables. This schema approach was chosen for its flexibility in supporting multiple business processes and analytical needs while maintaining data consistency across the warehouse. The central Fact Table in this Galaxy Schema is the SalesFact table, which captures transactional sales data at the most granular level. This table contains the primary measures including Sales (revenue from each transaction), Quantity (number of items purchased), Discount (percentage or fixed discount applied), and Profit (net profit from each transaction). The SalesFact table serves as the foundation for all sales-related analytics and connects to multiple dimension tables through foreign key relationships. The dimension tables include the CustomerDimension containing customer attributes such as CustomerID (primary key), CustomerName, Segment (Consumer, Corporate, or Home Office), Country, City, State, PostalCode, and Region. The ProductDimension table stores ProductID (primary key), Category (Furniture, Office Supplies, or Technology), SubCategory, and ProductName. The StoreDimension captures store information including StoreID, StoreName, Region, City, and State. The DateDimension provides a comprehensive calendar with DateKey, CalendarDate, Year, Quarter, Month, Day, Weekday, FiscalYear, and FiscalQuarter fields. Additionally, the ShipModeDimension contains shipping method options including Standard Class, Second Class, First Class, and Same Day. This Galaxy Schema design supports multiple analytical capabilities including analyzing sales by region to identify geographic performance patterns, analyzing sales by product category and subcategory to understand product portfolio performance, analyzing sales trends over time including yearly, quarterly, monthly, and daily patterns, analyzing customer segmentation to understand purchasing behavior across different customer types, and analyzing shipping mode preferences to optimize delivery logistics. The schema also supports data cube creation for multi-dimensional analysis combining region, time, product, and customer dimensions simultaneously.

### Implement the Database Schema

The database implementation utilizes Supabase, a PostgreSQL-based platform that provides enterprise-grade database capabilities with real-time features and an intuitive API. The implementation creates all necessary tables with proper primary key and foreign key constraints to maintain data integrity and support efficient querying. The SalesFact table is created with RowID as the primary key and includes foreign key references to CustomerDimension (CustomerID), ProductDimension (ProductID), DateDimension (OrderDateKey and ShipDateKey), and ShipModeDimension (ShipMode). The foreign key constraints ensure referential integrity by preventing invalid references to dimension records. Additional constraints include NOT NULL constraints on essential fields such as OrderID, OrderDate, Sales, Quantity, and Profit to ensure data quality. Dimension tables are similarly created with appropriate primary keys and data types. The DateDimension uses Integer for DateKey to enable efficient joins, while the CalendarDate field stores the full timestamp. The CustomerDimension and ProductDimension tables use TEXT data types for identification fields to maintain compatibility with source data formats. Indexes are created on frequently queried columns including foreign key fields and commonly filtered attributes such as OrderDate, Region, and Category to optimize query performance.

## ETL Process

The Extract, Transform, Load (ETL) pipeline is implemented using Apache NiFi, an old version that supports custom scripting capabilities. Apache NiFi was chosen for its visual workflow design interface, extensive processor library, and robust data flow management features. The NiFi implementation enables automated data ingestion from multiple sources, data transformation and cleansing, and loading into the Supabase data warehouse. The Extract phase utilizes multiple NiFi processors to retrieve data from different sources. The GetFile processor reads CSV dimension files from the designated input directory, while the QueryDatabaseTable processor extracts transactional data from the SQLite database. For Excel files, the GetExcel processor with the Excel Reader service parses workbook structures and extracts worksheet data. Each source is processed independently to ensure data integrity and enable error tracking at the source level. The Transform phase applies business rules and data quality transformations using processors including UpdateRecord for field modifications, ConvertRecord for data type conversions, and ExecuteScript for complex transformations using Python or Groovy scripts. Key transformations include parsing date strings into standard ISO format for the DateDimension, calculating derived fields such as total sales amount, standardizing customer and product identifiers across sources, and applying data type conversions to ensure compatibility with the target database schema. The Load phase writes transformed data to the Supabase data warehouse using the PutDatabaseRecord processor configured with appropriate JDBC connection pooling. Data is loaded into staging tables first to enable validation before merging into production tables. The Load process handles dimension tables first to ensure foreign key constraints are satisfied when loading fact table data. Error handling routes failed records to a separate flow for investigation and reprocessing.

## Data Analysis and Visualization

Power BI serves as the visualization platform for analyzing the Superstore data warehouse. The implementation includes ten comprehensive visualizations that provide insights into sales performance, customer behavior, product trends, and operational metrics. Each visualization is designed to support specific business decisions and enable drill-down analysis across multiple dimensions.

1. Sales by Region (Map Visualization): A geographic map displays total sales revenue color-coded by region across the United States. The visualization reveals that the West region generates the highest sales volume, followed by East, Central, and South regions. This insight enables regional managers to benchmark performance and identify expansion opportunities in underperforming areas. The map supports drill-down to state and city levels for granular geographic analysis.

2. Sales Trend Over Time (Line Chart): A line chart showing monthly sales trends from 2014 to 2017 reveals seasonal patterns and year-over-year growth trajectories. The visualization demonstrates consistent growth in sales volume with notable peaks during November and December, indicating strong holiday season performance. The trend line enables forecasting models to predict future sales and inform inventory planning decisions.

3. Profit by Category (Bar Chart): A horizontal bar chart displays profit margins across product categories including Furniture, Office Supplies, and Technology. The visualization highlights that Technology products generate the highest absolute profit despite lower sales volume, while Office Supplies contribute the highest volume but with lower margins. Furniture shows moderate performance across both metrics. This insight guides product mix decisions and pricing strategies.

4. Customer Segment Distribution (Pie Chart): A pie chart illustrates the distribution of sales across customer segments including Consumer, Corporate, and Home Office. Consumer segment dominates with approximately 50% of total sales, followed by Corporate at 30% and Home Office at 20%. This segmentation informs targeted marketing campaigns and customer relationship management strategies tailored to each segment preferences and purchasing patterns.

5. Top 10 Products by Sales (Bar Chart): A ranked bar chart displays the top ten products by total sales revenue, revealing best-selling items across the product portfolio. Analysis shows that technology products such as phones and computers dominate the top positions, indicating strong demand for electronic items. This information supports inventory optimization and identifies opportunities for bundling related products to increase average order value.

6. Sales by Ship Mode (Donut Chart): A donut chart displays the distribution of orders across shipping methods including Standard Class, Second Class, First Class, and Same Day. Standard Class accounts for the majority of shipments due to its cost-effectiveness, while Same Day shipping shows the lowest volume but highest revenue per order. This visualization helps logistics teams optimize shipping strategies and promotional offers for different delivery options.

7. Quarterly Revenue Growth (Area Chart): An area chart visualizes quarterly revenue trends demonstrating consistent growth patterns across fiscal quarters. The visualization reveals Q4 (October-December) consistently outperforms other quarters due to holiday season demand, while Q1 typically shows the lowest performance. This seasonal pattern informs workforce planning, inventory management, and marketing budget allocation throughout the fiscal year.

8. Sales by State (Map Visualization): A state-level choropleth map shows sales distribution across individual U.S. states, identifying California, New York, and Texas as top-performing states. The visualization reveals significant geographic concentration of sales in coastal states and metropolitan areas, suggesting opportunities for market expansion in underperforming midwestern states. State-level analysis supports regional marketing strategies and store location decisions.

9. Discount Impact on Profit (Scatter Plot): A scatter plot analysis examines the relationship between discount percentages and profit margins. The visualization demonstrates a clear negative correlation, showing that higher discounts consistently lead to reduced or negative profit margins. This insight guides pricing and promotional strategies to maintain profitability while remaining competitive. The analysis identifies optimal discount levels that maximize sales volume without sacrificing margin.

10. Sales by Segment and Category (Stacked Bar Chart): A stacked bar chart combines customer segments and product categories to reveal cross-dimensional insights. The visualization shows that Consumer segment purchases are dominated by Office Supplies, while Corporate and Home Office segments show higher Technology purchases. This cross-analysis enables targeted product recommendations and segment-specific marketing campaigns that align with purchasing preferences across different customer types.

### Discussion of Results

The ten visualizations collectively reveal several critical insights for Superstore business intelligence. Geographic analysis indicates significant revenue concentration in western and eastern coastal regions, with California, New York, and Texas accounting for a disproportionate share of total sales. Seasonal patterns demonstrate that Q4 consistently outperforms other quarters, suggesting the importance of holiday season planning and inventory optimization. Product category analysis reveals Technology products generate the highest profit margins despite lower volume, while Office Supplies drive the majority of transactional volume. Customer segmentation shows Consumer segment dominance, but Corporate and Home Office segments show higher-value purchasing patterns that merit targeted engagement strategies. The discount analysis provides particularly valuable insight showing the inverse relationship between discounting and profitability, enabling data-driven pricing decisions that optimize both volume and margin.

## Data Ethics

The analysis and use of business data in this data warehouse implementation raises several important ethical considerations that must be addressed to ensure responsible data practices and maintain customer trust. Data privacy represents a fundamental concern in this analysis, as the dataset contains personally identifiable information including customer names, addresses, and purchase histories. While this data supports valuable business analytics, organizations must ensure appropriate data protection measures including encryption, access controls, and compliance with regulations such as GDPR and CCPA. Customer data should be anonymized where possible, and data retention policies should limit storage duration to what is strictly necessary for business purposes. Bias in data analysis presents significant ethical challenges, as analytical results can inadvertently perpetuate or amplify existing biases in business operations. The customer segmentation analysis must avoid discriminatory practices based on demographic characteristics. Similarly, pricing and promotional strategies derived from discount analysis should not exploit vulnerable customer groups. Regular audits of analytical models and outputs help identify and mitigate potential biases that could lead to unfair treatment of certain customer segments. Transparency in data usage requires clear communication with customers about how their data is collected, used, and analyzed. Organizations should provide accessible privacy policies and obtain informed consent where appropriate. The insights generated from this data warehouse should be used to benefit both the organization and its customers through improved products, services, and experiences rather than manipulative practices that prioritize profit over customer welfare.
